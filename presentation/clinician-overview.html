Nurse-run clinics ARE the reality — nurses are making clinical decisions without adequate decision support. Digital alternatives exist — keyword-searchable drug formularies built for doctors — but formulary lookup ("what drug, what dose") is not clinical decision support ("what could this be, what should I rule out, when do I refer"). Nothing today gives nurses intelligent triage guidance at the point of care.

Clinical Safety

Clinical Safety — Five Layers

Patient safety in a clinical AI system requires defence in depth. No single layer can catch everything. CareMate uses five independent safety layers, each addressing a different failure mode.

Layer 1: SATS/TEWS Acuity

The South African Triage Scale (SATS) — the nationally standardised system used across all SA emergency departments. 100% deterministic, no LLM involved.

TEWS ScoreSATS ColourPriorityTarget Time
7+REDEmergencyImmediate
5–6ORANGEVery Urgent< 10 minutes
3–4YELLOWUrgent< 60 minutes
0–2GREENRoutine< 4 hours

TEWS scores 7 components (each 0–3 points): Heart Rate, Respiratory Rate, Systolic BP, Temperature, Consciousness (AVPU), Mobility, Trauma. Age-stratified thresholds for adult vs child. Clinical discriminators (e.g., active seizure, chest pain at rest) override TEWS and assign colour directly.

Layer 2: Care Level Boundaries

Tasleem's Rule

Primary STG = treat at PHC. Hospital EML = identify + refer. Specialist = flag + refer. The nurse needs to recognise hospital-level conditions so they can refer appropriately, but treatment is not in their scope.

15 referral-only conditions (9 O&G + 6 Hospital EML critical) with symptom edges + referral_required=TRUE. Triage FINDS them but says REFER, not TREAT.

Layer 3: Drug Safety

Medication Safety Checks

ACE inhibitors in pregnancy, NSAID + renal impairment, warfarin + NSAID interactions, CNS depressant stacking. Patient-specific, not generic warnings.

Layer 4: Safety Review Gate

Independent LLM Review

A separate LLM call reviews the complete triage output for missed dangers. Can only escalate, never downgrade. Speculation without evidence is capped at "priority". Runs in parallel with synthesis — zero added latency.

Layer 5: Clinical Opportunities Engine

25 Deterministic Rules

Proactive screening, diagnosis-triggered workups, vital sign incidentals, SDOH referrals, and medication safety alerts. Fires automatically based on patient data — no LLM, no hallucination risk.

Part 1: The Triage Agent

The Triage Agent

A patient arrives at the clinic. The nurse hears their complaint, takes their vitals, and needs to work out what’s wrong. In a busy SA primary care facility seeing 40–60 patients a day, this decision often happens under pressure, without a doctor on site, and with limited access to reference material.

The Triage Agent is CareMate’s answer to this moment. The nurse types the patient’s complaint, enters vitals, and within seconds receives a ranked differential diagnosis grounded entirely in the SA Standard Treatment Guidelines — complete with SATS acuity colour, danger signs to watch for, and targeted questions to narrow the diagnosis.

Why We Built It This Way

Most AI systems generate their answers from a language model — which means the answer can change unpredictably, and you can’t verify what knowledge it used. We took a different approach: we built a clinical knowledge graph — every condition, every symptom relationship, every danger sign extracted from the STG and stored as structured, validated data. The AI helps with understanding the nurse’s language, but the clinical matching, scoring, and safety checks are deterministic. The same inputs always produce the same outputs. This is what makes it auditable, testable, and safe.

Part 2: The Clinical Assistant

The Clinical Assistant

Mid-encounter, a nurse has questions. Can I give this drug to a pregnant patient? What dose of amoxicillin for a 4-year-old? The patient’s eGFR is 38 — should I refer? Today, the answer lives somewhere in a 747-page guideline book, if the nurse has time to find it.

The Clinical Assistant is an AI agent the nurse can ask these questions to during the encounter. It searches the SA Standard Treatment Guidelines and 6 other validated clinical sources, checks the answer against this specific patient’s context — their age, diagnosis, vitals, current medications — and responds with a cited, sourced answer. It remembers the conversation, so the nurse can ask follow-up questions naturally.

Why It’s an Autonomous Agent, Not a Search Engine

A search engine returns pages. An agent reasons. When a nurse asks “Can I give ibuprofen to this patient?”, the Clinical Assistant doesn’t just look up ibuprofen — it checks whether the patient is pregnant (contraindicated), whether they’re on an ACE inhibitor (dangerous interaction), whether they have renal impairment (dose adjustment needed), and if the drug is unsafe, it proactively finds an alternative. It chains multiple steps together autonomously, just as an experienced colleague would think through the problem. This is the difference between a tool that retrieves information and an agent that reasons about it.

Agent Architecture

The Clinical Assistant is built as a true AI agent with five core components — the same architecture used in modern autonomous AI systems, adapted for clinical safety:

Knowledge

Two layers: structured DB + 98 markdown files

Structured DB: 335 conditions, 12,150 clinical edges, 1,532 knowledge chunks — queryable, scored, validated.
Markdown KB: 98 files across 7 SA clinical sources — readable, editable, version-controlled. Not a black box — clinicians can open any file and see exactly what the agent knows.

Tools (9)

Atomic capabilities the agent can invoke

Each tool is a discrete function: search guidelines, check drug safety, draft a referral letter. The agent decides which tools to call and in what order based on the nurse’s question. Tools are the agent’s hands — how it retrieves, checks, and acts.

Memory

DB-persisted multi-turn conversations

Every message, tool call, and result is stored. The agent loads the last 10 turns into each call — it remembers what was asked, what it found, and what advice it gave. Full audit trail for clinical governance.

Context

Patient-specific encounter data injected per call

Every question is grounded in the current patient: demographics, working diagnosis, vitals, SATS triage colour, current prescriptions, and extracted symptoms. The agent doesn’t answer in the abstract — it answers for this patient.

Orchestration

Agentic loop — up to 4 rounds of reasoning

The LLM reasons about the question, calls one or more tools, evaluates results, and decides whether to call more tools or produce a final answer. Behaviour rules — care level boundaries, STG-first grounding, proactive safety flags — are defined as readable markdown documents the clinical team can review and approve.

Part 3: The Encounter Agent

The Encounter Agent

Documentation is where nurses lose time. A proper SOAP note, a care plan the patient can understand, a discharge summary for handover — in a high-volume clinic, this paperwork often gets rushed or skipped entirely. When it does get done, the patient care plan is typically in English, regardless of the patient’s home language, and written at a level many patients cannot follow.

The Encounter Agent generates all three documents automatically once the nurse confirms a diagnosis and prescribes treatment. The SOAP note meets SA legal requirements for a medical record. The patient care plan is written at Grade 8 literacy level in the patient’s own language — any of South Africa’s 11 official languages. Everything is grounded in the STG, print-ready, and generated in 2–3 seconds.

Why This Matters Clinically

Research consistently shows that patients who understand their care plan — what medicines to take, what danger signs to watch for, when to return — have better outcomes and fewer unnecessary re-presentations. A care plan in isiZulu at Grade 8 literacy is not a nice-to-have; for a patient in a rural KZN clinic, it may be the difference between following up appropriately and not. Dr Tasleem Ras has validated that the SOAP note “meets all of the legal requirements” for a South African medical record.

SOAP Note

Structured clinical note for the medical record. Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan — all grounded in STG with guideline citations. Meets SA legal requirements for a medical record (validated by Dr Tasleem Ras).

Patient Care Plan

Patient-facing discharge instructions at Grade 8 literacy level. Medication instructions, lifestyle advice, danger signs, follow-up schedule. Available in all 11 SA official languages. Print-ready with signature lines.

Discharge Summary

Clinician-facing handover document. Presenting complaint, diagnosis, treatment given, follow-up plan, safety netting. Automatically flags if referral is indicated per STG criteria.
